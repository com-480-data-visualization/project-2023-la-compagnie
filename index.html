About.

Glacier Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs) are a natural hazard that occurs when a glacier-dammed lake suddenly empties due to the failure of the ice or moraine that blocks its outlet. GLOFs are typically triggered by a combination of factors such as intense rainfall, rapid glacier melting, or earthquakes that cause the dam to rupture, leading to a sudden release of water and debris downstream.

The sudden discharge of water from a glacier lake can result in catastrophic floods that can cause extensive damage to infrastructure, property, and loss of human lives. The water can travel at high speeds, carrying with it large boulders, sediment, and other debris, leading to significant erosion and destruction of the landscape.

GLOFs are common in mountainous regions, particularly in the Himalayas and Andes, where glaciers are abundant. Climate change has led to an increase in the frequency and magnitude of GLOFs, as glaciers melt at an accelerating rate. This trend is expected to continue in the future, posing significant risks to communities living downstream from glacier lakes.

Effective monitoring and early warning systems are crucial in mitigating the impacts of GLOFs. In addition, proactive measures such as the construction of spillways or controlled drainage systems can help prevent or reduce the damage caused by GLOFs.

Recorded GLOFs

The dataset consists of 3457 glacier lake outburst floods, recorded all over the world, during a large time span.

Century

The oldest recorded GLOF in the dataset is from the 13th century. The glacier lake outburst flood happened in Iceland on the Skeiðarárjökull glacier.

Average yearly increase of GLOFs

The number of glacier lake outburst floods increases with 11.8% every year. This might be explained by more thorough measurements and climate change.
